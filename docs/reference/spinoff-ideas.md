# Zombo — Creative Spin-off Proposals

Two distinct direction proposals for Zombo's identity. Each builds on PvZ's proven lane-based tower defence foundation but diverges into its own world with unique mechanics that PvZ doesn't have.

---

## Proposal 1: "Critter Kitchen"

### Concept pitch

Instead of plants vs. zombies, it's **kitchen creations vs. hungry critters**. The player is a kid chef defending their epic birthday feast from waves of adorable but ravenous backyard animals (raccoons, possums, seagulls, ants, neighbourhood cats). Defenders are food items placed on a long picnic table grid — hot sauce bottles that spray, jelly walls that wobble and bounce critters back, popcorn mines that explode into a scatter, and a garden hose that freezes into an ice cream stream at night. The sun economy is replaced by a "flavour" economy where the player collects falling flavour drops to cook up new dishes.

### Unique differentiating mechanics

1. **Recipe combiner** — Two adjacent food defenders can be "cooked" together to create a combo dish with new abilities. For example, placing a Chilli next to a Popcorn creates Chilli Popcorn that fires in a spread pattern. This introduces a spatial puzzle layer PvZ doesn't have: placement order and adjacency matter for creating powerful combos. There are 10+ discoverable recipes, encouraging experimentation across playthroughs.

2. **Critter mood system** — Enemy critters have moods (hungry, sleepy, angry, curious) that change their behaviour mid-wave. A sleepy raccoon walks slowly but takes more hits to scare off; an angry seagull dive-bombs from above, skipping the lane entirely. The player can see mood indicators and must adapt tactics in real-time, adding a reaction/read layer beyond PvZ's predictable zombie patterns.

3. **Messy aftermath scoring** — After each wave, the table is rated on "messiness" (splatter, crumbs, toppled items). Higher mess = higher score multiplier, but also attracts tougher critters next wave. Players choose between playing clean (easier next wave, lower score) or going wild (harder next wave, higher score). This risk-reward loop adds replayability and lets kids find their own difficulty sweet spot.

4. **Co-op picnic mode** — Two players defend a double-wide table, each controlling their half but sharing a flavour pool. They can toss ingredients to each other across the table centre. Designed for siblings or parent-child play on the same device.

### Target audience

Kids 5-10, with co-op mode designed for sibling pairs or parent-child play. The food/cooking theme is universally appealing and avoids the "are zombies scary?" question entirely.

### Why kids will love it

Combining food items to discover surprise recipes feels like a science experiment at the dinner table — every new combo is a "whoa, what does THIS do?" moment that rewards curiosity and makes each playthrough feel different.

## Proposal 2: "Toy Box Siege"

### Concept pitch

The battle takes place inside a kid's messy bedroom, where **toy defenders** protect a blanket fort from waves of **dust bunnies, lost socks, and cleaning robots** sent by Mum to tidy up. Defenders are toys: action figures that punch, water pistols that shoot, building block walls, RC cars that patrol lanes, and a disco ball that stuns with flashing lights. The economy uses "imagination sparks" that float up from the fort — the more creative the defence, the more sparks it generates. Each world is a different room in the house (bedroom, living room, garage, garden shed, attic), each with unique terrain like carpet/tile/grass that affects toy movement.

### Unique differentiating mechanics

1. **Toy battery system** — Every active toy defender drains from a shared battery pool that slowly recharges. Cheap toys (rubber duck decoy, building block wall) drain nothing, but powerful toys (RC car, laser pointer turret) drain fast. The player must balance their army between "always-on" passive toys and "burst" active toys that they toggle on/off. This creates a real-time resource management layer PvZ doesn't have — it's not just about what you place, but when you activate it.

2. **Room rotation** — Every 3 waves, the camera "rotates" and enemies come from a different edge of the room (left, right, top, bottom). The grid stays the same but the attack direction changes, so a defence optimised for left-to-right suddenly faces top-to-bottom. Players must build symmetric or adaptable layouts rather than the single-direction focus PvZ allows. This transforms the strategic puzzle from "build a wall on the right" to "build a fortress that works from any angle."

3. **Mess meter and Mum's ultimatum** — A "mess meter" fills as battles rage (explosions scatter toy parts, water puddles spread). If it hits 100%, Mum arrives for a "tidy-up timeout" — a brief pause where the player must frantically tap to clean up messes before the next wave starts. If they clean enough, they get a bonus toy from the toy box. If they don't, Mum confiscates their most expensive deployed toy. This creates tension peaks between waves and a clean-up mini-game that gives fingers something to do during downtime.

4. **Toy Box drafting** — At the start of each level, the player draws 8 toys from a randomised toy box (like a card draft). They can re-draw once by "putting toys back." This means every playthrough has a different loadout, forcing adaptation and preventing a single dominant strategy. Rare toys (gold-star sticker on the card) appear occasionally and have unique abilities.

### Target audience

Kids 6-12. The bedroom/toy theme resonates universally — every kid has imagined their toys coming alive. Slightly older skew than Proposal 1 due to the battery management and room rotation mechanics requiring more strategic thinking.

### Why kids will love it

The room rotation mechanic creates genuine "oh no, they're coming from BEHIND!" panic moments that feel like an action movie — and the mess meter means even cleaning up becomes a frantic, giggly mini-game instead of a chore.
